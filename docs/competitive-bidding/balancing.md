# Balancing

Summary of balancing actions:

1. A takeout double may be made with as few as 9 HCP, but only with shortness in the opponent’s suit. Partner should consider this possibility when responding.
2. A balancing 1NT shows 11–15 HCP and a balanced hand. Stayman and transfers apply.
3. A balancing 2NT shows 19–21 HCP and a balanced hand. Stayman and transfers apply.
4. With 16–18 HCP and a balanced hand, double first and then bid NT over partner’s response.
5. A jump-shift (even non-vulnerable) is constructive, showing a good 6-card suit and 11–15 HCP.

## Balancing after (1H)-Pass-(2H)-Pass-Pass-?

We have simulated the balancing position after the opponents open 1H and raise to 2H. The main conclusion is that shape and landing place matter more than raw strength. HLD helps, but only when the hand has the right structure. Do not balance just because you have points; balance because your hand can stand the contracts we are likely to reach.

### Agreements after a takeout double

After double, partner responds approximately as follows:

- 2S = 4+ spades
- 3D = 5+ diamonds
- 3C = 4+ clubs
- 2NT = asks doubler to choose a minor

If partner responds 3C and doubler has 4+ spades and 5+ diamonds, doubler corrects to 3D. This means that a double with 4 spades and 5 diamonds does not need to tolerate 3C as the final contract.

### Main rule

Balance on shape, not on points. Good balancing hands have 5+ spades, 4 spades for a takeout double, or clear minor-suit structure. Weak hands without a safe landing place should often pass, especially vulnerable.

### Guidelines for takeout double

With 4 spades, double is normally the best balancing call. Non-vulnerable, about 8.5+ HLD is often enough if the hand also has reasonable minor-suit preparedness. Vulnerable, be more cautious: about 10.5-12+ HLD. With 4 spades and 5+ diamonds, double is especially attractive because we can correct partner's 3C response to 3D.

With 3 spades, double is possible but requires a better hand. Non-vulnerable, about 10.5+ HLD; vulnerable, prefer about 12.5+ HLD. The hand should have good minor-suit tolerance, especially club preparedness, because partner may respond 3C with only a four-card suit.

With 2 spades, we should normally not double. It requires strong special hands, about 14.5+ HLD, and good minor-suit structure. The risk is that partner responds 2S and we land in a bad spade fit.

With 0-1 spades, we should basically not make a takeout double. Choose 2NT, a suit bid, or pass depending on the hand.

### Direct bids

2S shows 5+ spades. It can be bid fairly light, especially non-vulnerable. With 6+ spades, the bid is very attractive. With only 4 spades, we should normally not bid 2S; double is better if the hand is suitable.

2NT shows the minors. With 5-5 in the minors, 2NT is very attractive. With 5-4 or 4-5, 2NT is often good non-vulnerable but more selective vulnerable. With only 4-4 in the minors, it requires extras and the right hand type; it is not a routine bid. 4-4 minors with two hearts looks especially doubtful.

A direct 3m should normally show a 6+ card suit. With only a five-card minor, you need extras, a good suit, and about 14+ HLD, especially vulnerable. A five-card minor without extra playing strength is dangerous because we can often be doubled when the contract goes down.

### Matchpoints

At matchpoints we are not necessarily less active, but shape becomes even more important. It is good to fight for the part-score when we have a safe landing place, but bad three-level contracts and bad 2S fits are expensive. 2S with 5+ spades and double with 4 spades hold up well. Thin 3m bids and doubles with 0-2 spades become clearly worse.

### Practical memory rule

- Bid 2S with 5+ spades.
- Double with 4 spades, or with 3 spades and good minor-suit tolerance.
- Bid 2NT with real minors, preferably at least 5-4.
- Bid 3m with a 6+ card suit, not lightly on a five-card suit.
- Pass hands without a clear landing place, even if they have some points.
